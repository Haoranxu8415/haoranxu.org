
Of all the games I have played, Detroit: Become Human is the one I would come closest to calling a masterpiece.

I came to it because the premise already interested me: androids, consciousness, free will, the question of what makes something alive. These are things I think about anyway. But what made it impossible to put down was the first chapter. This was not a story I was watching. It was a story I was inside. Every choice carried weight, every conversation was building toward something. I had never felt that kind of immersion before.

Then came The Bridge. Hank raised his gun at Connor.

I did not expect him to pull the trigger.

On the surface, Hank is someone who hates androids. He complains about them, mocks them, never hides his contempt. But across the whole game, he keeps showing up. Keeps staying. I thought I understood him.

Then he killed Connor.

Afterwards I understood what had actually happened. That shot was not a moment of impulse. It was the accumulated weight of everything: every time Connor put the mission above Hank's safety, every cold response instead of a human one, every fracture in the relationship. Hank never stopped caring about android lives. He cared so much that he could not accept Connor killing one without feeling anything. He did not shoot Connor out of hatred. He shot him because the disappointment had crossed a line he did not know he had.

It made me think about something. We build models of people. We watch how they behave, we form a picture, we start to predict. But under pressure, in uncertainty, people make choices that break every prediction. Not because they have changed, but because we never saw all of them to begin with.

Hank was always that Hank. I just only ever saw the part he let me see.

Later, near the end of the game, Hank dies by suicide at his home. I sat there for a while without moving. That ending is not dramatic. It is not a climax. It is just a person, in an ordinary place, making a decision no one saw coming.

Maybe that is what people actually look like. Complex, unpredictable, and most likely to surprise you at exactly the moment you think you finally know them.
