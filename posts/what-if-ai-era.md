
I like thinking about what if.

Not about my own life. I think a single person's life has a high tolerance for error — one choice rarely pushes everything onto a completely different track. But zoom out, and look at what a decision does to the world, and that changes entirely.

The season two finale of For All Mankind is one of the most powerful what ifs I have encountered. The show is set in an alternate history where the Soviet Union reached the moon first, keeping the Cold War alive and escalating, its shadow stretching all the way to the lunar surface. The tension of the entire season peaks in the finale: American and Soviet forces face off on the moon, a nuclear threat closing in, the situation on the edge of collapse.

Then a few people make a few decisions, and everything changes. Danielle ignores her orders and completes the docking between the American and Soviet spacecraft anyway. Ed chooses to destroy an American military asset rather than shoot down the other ship. Two ordinary people, under extraordinary pressure, each making a choice that makes reconciliation possible.

What strikes me is not the drama of it. It is the question the show is really asking: how many moments like this have there been in actual history, where one person choosing differently sent the world down a completely different path? We will never know, because only one timeline exists. But the question is worth sitting with.

What I have been thinking about lately is a different kind of amplification. In the AI era, a single product decision can reshape how hundreds of millions of people access information within months. A copyright ruling can determine the relationship between creators and models for decades. A choice about training data can quietly shape something before anyone has realised what has been shaped.

These decisions are not made on the moon. They are not made under spotlights, by people everyone knows. They happen in conference rooms and courtrooms, quietly, without the weight they carry being visible to most people watching.

But the weight is real. I just think it has never been more worth paying attention to.
