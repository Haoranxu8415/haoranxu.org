Research · LLM

Visual Dark Patterns & AI Decision-Making

It started with Information Bubbles. Building that project made me think seriously, for the first time, about the relationship between algorithms and attention — how platforms quietly make choices on your behalf, through design, through sequencing, through what they choose to show you first.

Over the winter break, I found myself thinking one layer deeper. AI shopping agents are now capable of browsing products and generating purchase recommendations on behalf of consumers. These agents are multimodal — they receive text and images simultaneously, just like a human shopper would. That means they are exposed to the same visual tactics that e-commerce platforms have spent years optimising for human psychology: inflated strikethrough prices, countdown timers, artificial scarcity signals.

Existing research has shown that text-based price anchoring can influence large language model outputs. But does the visual presentation of that information, independent of its content, produce an additional effect? That question, as far as I can tell, remains unanswered.

That is what I set out to investigate: whether visual dark patterns can systematically shift multimodal LLM evaluations under controlled conditions, and whether defensive prompting can meaningfully counter that influence. The research is actively in progress, with plans to submit to an academic journal.
